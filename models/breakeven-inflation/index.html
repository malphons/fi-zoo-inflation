BEI Decomposition

Breakeven inflation is not a pure measure of expected inflation. It includes a risk premium and a liquidity premium (TIPS are less liquid than nominals).

BEI = Expected Inflation + Inflation Risk Premium − TIPS Liquidity Premium

Key insight: When TIPS liquidity is poor (e.g., during crises), the liquidity premium widens, making breakevens understate true inflation expectations. Conversely, a positive risk premium means breakevens overstate expected inflation.

Forward Breakeven Rates

Forward breakevens strip out near-term inflation to reveal what the market expects for inflation between two future dates. The 5Y5Y forward is the most watched measure by central banks.

The 5Y5Y forward breakeven is the Fed’s preferred measure of long-run inflation expectations. It captures what the market expects inflation to average from year 5 to year 10 — stripping out transitory near-term effects.

Understanding Breakeven Inflation

What It Is

The breakeven inflation rate is the spread between a nominal Treasury yield and a TIPS (real) yield at the same maturity. It is the inflation rate at which an investor is indifferent between holding the nominal bond and the inflation-linked bond.

How to Trade It

  • Buy breakevens: Buy TIPS, sell nominals. Profits if realized inflation exceeds the BEI rate.
  • Sell breakevens: Sell TIPS, buy nominals. Profits if realized inflation is below the BEI rate.
  • Inflation swaps: Achieve similar exposure without balance sheet constraints.

Pitfalls & Nuances

  • Liquidity premium: TIPS are less liquid; their yields include a premium, biasing BEI down.
  • Risk premium: Investors demand compensation for inflation uncertainty, biasing BEI up.
  • CPI vs. PCE: TIPS are linked to CPI, but the Fed targets PCE. The historical CPI–PCE wedge is ~30 bps.
